Equations of Planes:

You may have geometric intuition that three points determine a plane. Our equations for planes will be built using one point, P0, and vector that is perpendicular to the plane, called the normal vector: n→.


Figure 1: Illustration of a plane containing two vectors, and a vector that is perpendicular to the plane.

 

Note that if we have three points that are on the plane, P,Q,R, we can ﬁnd the normal direction by taking the cross product of vectors drawn from one point to the other two points, for example: n→ = PR⃗ ×PQ⃗.

The plane equations are built on two key ideas: the normal vector, n→, is perpendicular to any vector that lies in the plane, and that the dot product of two perpendicular vectors is zero.

Let r→0 = x0,y0,z0 be the position vector for a speciﬁc point on the plane, P0 = (x0,y0,z0), and let r→ = x,y,z be the position vector for any general point on the plane: P = (x,y,z). Then the vector P0P⃗ = r→ −r→0 = x − x0,y − y0,z − z0 lies in the plane. Finally, let n→ = a,b,c be the normal vector. Then the plane can be described by any of the following types of equations:


Figure 2: Illustration of a plane in three dimensions for reference when building plane equations.

 

Vector Equation:

n→ ⋅ (r→ −r→0) = 0

 

Scalar Equation:

a(x − x0) + b(y − y0) + c(z − z0) = 0

 

Standard Form:

ax + by + cz + d = 0

 

Note that a,b,c are sometimes called the attitude numbers of the plane.
